ABSTRACT
As urban rail transit shifts from rapid expansion to quality-oriented
development, understanding the coordination between metro net-
works and urbanization has become critical for urban planning.
Existing studies mainly focus on single-city cases or static spa-
tial snapshots, providing limited support for analyzing the dy-
namic coupling between metro development and urban growth
across cities and time. We present a visual analytics system for
metro–urbanization coupling analysis. The system integrates multi-
source spatiotemporal data, including metro lines and stations, pop-
ulation distribution, points of interest, building density, and GDP.
Based on the entropy weight method, it constructs a metro devel-
opment index and an urbanization development index, and further
applies a coupling coordination model to quantify their relation-

matches, temporal changes, and potential optimization areas be-
tween metro construction and urban development. This work con-
tributes a visual analytics framework and design implications for
metro–urbanization coupling analysis in urban rail transit planning.

1 INTRODUCTION
Against the backdrop of accelerating global urbanization, Urban
Rail Transit (URT) systems, guided by Transit-Oriented Develop-
ment (TOD) theory, have evolved beyond mere solutions to traf-
fic congestion. They now serve as the critical backbone for shap-
ing compact urban spatial layouts, achieving jobs-housing balance,
and promoting mixed land use (Calthorpe, 1993; Calthorpe et al.,
2014). Currently, China’s URT construction is undergoing a strate-


--- PAGE 2 ---
gic transformation from “scale expansion” to “quality improve-
ment” (Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural Development, 2015;
Wang, 2016). The bidirectional interaction and dynamic coupling
between rail transit networks and urban population distribution,
land use, and economic activities have become a core research topic
in urban planning and policy evaluation.
Existing research and planning practices exhibit significant lim-
itations when exploring the coupling relationship between rail
transit and urbanization. Most literature focuses on static cross-
sectional analyses of single cities at specific time points (Dong et
al., 2013; Yao & Xiong, 2013), making it difficult to capture the dy-
namic laws of urban morphological evolution over long time series.
Although the Coupling Coordination Degree (CCD) model has ma-
tured in quantitative evaluation (Liao, 1999; Liu et al., 2005), it still
lacks intuitive diagnostic tools to assist planners in identifying spa-
tial mismatches and evaluating policy implementation effects when
processing multi-source, multi-scale, and multi-city heterogeneous
data.
To address these research gaps, this paper designs and imple-
ments the MetroCouple visual analytics system. Rather than focus-
ing on blind future predictions, the system is dedicated to “current
status diagnosis” and “historical evaluation”, providing an intuitive
decision support platform for urban morphology researchers and
policymakers.
2 RELATEDWORK
This study explores how to effectively characterize the dynamic
multi-scale coupling relationship between rail transit networks and
urbanization processes through visual analytics, to support current
status diagnosis and historical evaluation.
2.1 Coupling Research on Rail Transit and Urbanization
TOD theory is the cornerstone for understanding the interaction be-
tween rail transit and cities.Calthorpe (1993)first proposed the
core TOD framework, emphasizing compact, mixed land use de-
velopment centered on public transit stations. As China’s urbaniza-
tion enters the stock development stage, TOD theory has continu-
ously evolved during its localization, placing greater emphasis on
the reshaping effect of rail transit networks on urban spatial struc-
ture (Bian, 2009; Pan, 2008; Calthorpe et al., 2014).
In terms of quantitative evaluation, the CCD model is widely
used to measure the interaction intensity between the rail transit
subsystem and the urban development subsystem.Liao (1999)and
Liu et al. (2005)established the standard calculation framework
for this model, and subsequent scholars have conducted extensive
empirical studies on specific cities. For example,Dong et al. (2013)
analyzed the coupling coordination between rail transit and land use
in Shanghai, whileYao and Xiong (2013)explored the coordinated
development path in Beijing. However, most existing studies rely
on static statistical cross-sectional data, making it challenging to
reveal the dynamic evolutionary logic over long time series.
2.2 Visual Analytics of Urban Spatiotemporal Data
In the field of urban morphological evolution,Liu et al. (2016)
proposed the SmartAdp system, which integrates multi-source data
to enable in-depth analysis of urban dynamics. Regarding the cor-
relation between human mobility and urban spatial structure, the
InclusiViz system developed byYu et al. (2025)demonstrates how
visualization techniques can reveal urban segregation and mobility
relationships.V on Landesberger et al. (2015)used spatiotemporal
graphs and clustering techniques to explore the dynamic character-
istics of large-scale mobility data.
Despite breakthroughs in the interactive exploration of spa-
tiotemporal data, there remains a scarcity of visualization research
specifically targeting the “coupling process” between rail transit
and urbanization in urban planning scenarios. Existing systems
mostly focus on single-dimensional mobility patterns or spatial dis-
tributions, lacking intuitive representations of the collaborative evo-
lution of the two subsystems (Andrienko & Andrienko, 2006; Kraak
& Ormeling, 2010). In particular, significant research gaps exist in
cross-city horizontal comparisons and the mining of historical pat-
terns over long periods (more than 20 years).
2.3 Interpretive Models and Multi-Scale Analysis
With the application of artificial intelligence in urban research, en-
hancing model interpretability has become a new trend in visual
analytics. The What-If Tool developed byWexler et al. (2019)
allows users to understand model behavior through interactive sim-
ulation, while the VBridge system proposed byCheng et al. (2021)
demonstrates how to connect features and data to explain complex
predictive models.
In urban planning, model explainability directly impacts the sci-
entific nature of decision-making. Urban planning practices have
shown that due to the highly uneven development of urban re-
gions, pure prediction functions often lack academic rationality
(Gonzalez-Navarro & Turner , 2018). Accurate diagnosis of the cur-
rent status and policy evaluation of historical evolution are more
valuable. For example, the U.S. rail transit impact assessment sys-
tem represented byCervero and Landis (1997)emphasizes the im-
portance of quantitatively measuring the interaction between rail
transit and land development using multi-source geographic data,
and numerous subsequent studies have verified the effectiveness of
this approach (Bowes & Ihlanfeldt, 2001; Duncan, 2011). How-
ever, traditional calculation methods often rely on static statistical
tables, making it difficult to present the complex evolution of urban
morphology over long time series.
3 SYSTEMDESIGN ANDFUNCTIONALMODULES
The system design is oriented towards the practical needs of urban
planning, transforming TOD theory and the CCD model into an
interactive visual diagnostic tool.
3.1 Requirements Analysis and Design Goals
Early literature reviews (Liu et al., 2016; Yu et al., 2025) indicate
that existing visualization tools for rail-urban coupling coordination
lack capabilities in long-term, multi-city coupling analysis. Ac-
cordingly, this system establishes three core Design Goals (DGs):
•DG1: Cross-scale station catchment correlation analysis.
The station catchment is the most direct unit through which
rail transit influences urban space. The system must break the
traditional single-city scale and support hierarchical drilling
from “city-wide overview—specific line—microscopic sta-
tion catchment”. In particular, it should provide coupling di-
agnosis capabilities with 100m precision for the core “walka-
ble pocket” range (typically 300 800m) in TOD planning, to
identify differences in development efficiency across differ-
ent station catchments (Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural
Development, 2015; Liu, 2017).
•DG2: Dynamic retrospective of long-term coupling evolu-
tion.Urban development and rail transit construction exhibit
significant temporal lag effects (Cervero & Landis, 1997;
Zhang et al., 2019). The system must support annual retro-
spective from 1990 to 2025, enabling the evaluation of the
long-term actual contribution of past planning decisions (such
as the route selection of a specific line) to station catchment
development through spatiotemporal backtracking.
•DG3: Transparent diagnosis of multi-source heteroge-
neous indicators.The system must integrate heterogeneous

data on population, economy, transportation, and social vital-
ity, helping planners decompose the contribution rate of sub-
systems through interactive means, and realizing the transfor-
mation from “phenomenon discovery” to “cause analysis”.
Based on these three goals, our system not only focuses on pre-
senting coupling results but also emphasizes tracing the historical
evolution logic and diagnosing the current status of imbalance, pro-
viding new insights for urban morphology research and policy eval-
uation.
3.2 Theoretical Background and Core Model Logic
3.2.1 Station Catchment as the Core Research Object
Under the TOD theoretical framework, the station catchment is
not only a transportation node but also a catalyst for urban vital-
ity (Calthorpe, 1993). This system defines the station catchment as
a composite spatial unit within walking distance centered on a rail
transit station.
Drawing on the Node-Place Model, the system decomposes sta-
tion catchment attributes into transportation attributes (Node) and
land use attributes (Place), corresponding to the tension relation-
ship between the rail transit subsystem (U 1) and the urbanization
subsystem (U2) (Wang & Zhou, 2021). To achieve precise measure-
ment at the station catchment scale, the system abandons adminis-
trative boundary restrictions and uses 100m-resolution grids as the
basic spatial unit, enabling the capture of subtle differences within
station catchments (such as between areas near entrances/exits and
edge plots).
3.2.2 Coupling Coordination Degree Logic Layer
The system quantifies the interaction level of the “rail tran-
sit—urbanization” system through the CCD model:
•Subsystem construction:The rail transit subsystem (U 1)
measures line coverage and station density within the station
catchment. The urbanization subsystem (U2) integrates popu-
lation density (LandScan), output intensity (GDP), and func-
tional mix (POI).
•Dynamic weighting logic:The entropy weight method
is used to weight multi-dimensional indicators (Sun et al.,
2012), automatically identifying the factors that contribute
most to system coordination in different years. For example,
in the early stage of station catchment development, the high
variation in building density leads to an increased weight; in
the mature stage, POI richness becomes the core evaluation
indicator.
•Coupling evaluation formula:Calculate the coupling degree
Cand coordination indexTfor each grid, and finally generate
the coupling coordination degreeD(Liao, 1999).
3.3 System Functional Module Design
The MetroCouple system is divided into four highly synergistic
functional modules, corresponding to four interactive behaviors:
“spatial positioning, parameter control, time retrospective, and in-
depth analysis”.
3.3.1 Coupling Situation Map Module
The map module is the core visual container of the system, re-
sponsible for presenting the coupling status of each city and sta-
tion catchment. The system supports dynamic switching between
1km macro grids (for city-wide situation observation) and 100m
micro grids (for specific station catchment diagnosis). This design
responds to the requirements of multi-source data fusion visualiza-
tion proposed byLiu et al. (2016), effectively solving the problem
of data noise interference at the station catchment scale through
multi-scale layered rendering.
3.3.2 Interactive Control Panel
This module carries the flexibility of the system, allowing experts
to adjust analysis parameters for different scenarios. The sys-
tem integrates six representative rail transit cities: Beijing, Shang-
hai, Guangzhou, Shenzhen, Hangzhou, and Chengdu. These cities
cover different development stages and TOD implementation paths,
providing diverse analytical backgrounds for system evaluation
(Cao & Lin, 2008). Beijing and Shanghai represent mature megac-
ities, Shenzhen and Hangzhou embody rapidly growing rail tran-
sit cities, and Chengdu reflects the development characteristics of
western expanding cities. Users can place cities at different de-
velopment stages in the same coordinate system to observe differ-
ences in station catchment coupling patterns under different eco-
nomic backgrounds (such as mature Shanghai vs. rapidly expand-
ing Chengdu).
3.3.3 Long-Term Playback Timeline
The timeline module is designed as the main axis driving system
state changes, supporting annual retrospective from 2000 to 2035.
By dragging the timeline, users can intuitively observe the “climb-
ing” process of coupling coordination degree in surrounding station
catchments over time after the opening of a new line (Cervero &
Landis, 1997), and also identify station catchments that “have rail
transit but remain in a low coupling state for a long time”. This
long-term visual analysis plays an important role in evaluating the
scientific nature of past urban planning and predicting future devel-
opment potential.
3.3.4 Detailed Data Diagnosis Panel
The diagnosis panel aims to eliminate the “black box” feeling of al-
gorithms and provide quantitative decision-making basis for plan-
ners (Wexler et al., 2019; Cheng et al., 2021). When a user clicks
on a specific station catchment on the map, the panel automatically
generates subsystem contributions. IfU1 ≫U 2, the system prompts
that the area belongs to the “transportation-led type” and needs to
strengthen land development guidance; ifU 2 ≫U 1, it prompts the
“demand overload type” and needs to optimize transportation con-
nections. This data-based automatic prompt directly improves the
practicality of the system in the actual planning workflow.
3.4 Visual Communication and Interaction Design
3.4.1 Visual Style and Color System
MetroCouple adopts a minimalist 2D visual style with a dark black
background, highlighting the spatial distribution law of coupling
relationships with low visual noise. The overall interface uses dark
blue-gray black as the main tone, creating a calm atmosphere simi-
lar to a large-screen digital management interface; core data indica-
tors are presented in high-contrast cyan-blue/purple luminous fonts,
forming a sharp contrast with the dark background and facilitating
planners to quickly capture key values from a distance.
The color semantics of the system are concentrated in the cou-
pling coordination degree color spectrum design. Based on the clas-
sification system ofLiao (1999), the system divides CCD values
into five levels, corresponding to a progressive color spectrum from
warm to cold: extreme imbalance is marked in deep red, barely co-
ordinated in orange-yellow, initially coordinated in grass green, and
high-quality coordinated in cyan-blue to dark blue.

ban elements. The top layer is the rail transit network element,
which includes three independently controllable sub-layers:

4 RAILTRANSITSTATIONCATCHMENTDATAPROCESSING
PIPELINE ANDALGORITHMIMPLEMENTATION
This chapter elaborates on the technical implementation scheme
of the MetroCouple system. The system constructs an automated
data pipeline covering raw data input to coupling indicator out-
put. Taking the station catchment as the core spatial unit, it real-
izes the quantitative measurement of the interaction between urban
rail transit and urbanization through spatial standardization, multi-
dimensional indicator extraction, dynamic weighting, and coupling
modeling.
4.1 Mathematical Construction and Definition of Station
Catchment Spatial Units
The station catchment is the basic spatial measurement unit for
TOD coupling analysis. To overcome the defect that traditional
circular buffers cannot reflect the real walking environment, this
system uses Network Isochrone as the mathematical representation
of the station catchment.
4.1.1 Implementation of Walking Isochrone Algorithm
Based on the “15-minute community life circle” theory (Ministry
of Housing and Urban-Rural Development, 2015), the system uses
graph theory algorithms to search for travel costs in the topological
road network:
•Road network graph construction:Use OSMnx to obtain
urban vector road networks from OpenStreetMap and con-
struct a weighted graphG= (V,E)(Boeing, 2017).
•Reachable area calculation:Take the station coordinates
as the starting nodev 0, and use the Dijkstra algorithm to
extract the covered node set under the set walking speed
Vwalk =1.2m/sand time thresholdT=900s(Karner , 2018).
•Geometry generation:Perform Alpha-Shape operation on
the node set to generate a closed polygonS station. This def-
inition method can more truly reflect the actual radiation ef-
ficiency of stations to surrounding plots than Euclidean dis-
tance (Shen et al., 2018; Wang & Zhou, 2021).
4.1.2 Normalization of Spatial Scales
Due to the scale conflict between LandScan population data (1km
resolution) and building density data (100m resolution), the data
pipeline performs raster reprojection and resampling. According to
the suggestions ofLiu et al. (2016)in multi-source urban data fu-
sion, the system aligns all rasters to the WGS 84 coordinate system
and uniformly resamples them into 100m×100m grids to adapt to
microscopic diagnosis at the station catchment scale.
4.2 Multi-Dimensional Feature Extraction of Station
Catchments
The second stage of the data pipeline is to extract the feature vectors
of the rail transit subsystem (U 1) and the urbanization subsystem
(U2) from the station catchment polygons.

4.2.1 Rail Transit Development Level Indicators (U 1)
•Road network penetration rate:Calculate the ratio of the
total road length to the area within the station catchment, re-
flecting the walking network support of the station catchment.
•Rail service intensity:Count the number of lines and their
operating years within the station catchment. The calculation
logic refers to the research on the long-term anchoring effect
of rail transit on land byCervero and Landis (1997), com-
bined with the measurement idea of node centrality in com-
plex networks (Chen et al., 2017).
4.2.2 Urbanization Comprehensive Level Indicators (U 2)
•Population carrying intensity:Use the Zonal Statistics al-
gorithm to project LandScan raster population onto station
catchment polygons, and calculate the accurate total popula-
tion using the area-weighted allocation method (Rose et al.,
2021).
•Socio-economic vitality:Integrate Chinese Academy of Sci-
ences GDP rasters and Amap POI data. The functional mix
is calculated using the Shannon entropy formula to measure
the balance of POI categories such as commerce and resi-
dence, reflecting the functional diversity of the station catch-
ment (Frank & Pivo, 1994; Huston et al., 2015).
•Physical development intensity:Extract the average build-
ing coverage rate within the station catchment using 100m
building density rasters, reflecting the land development in-
tensity.

4.3 Objective Dynamic Weighting Algorithm Based on
Entropy Weight Method
To eliminate the subjective bias of artificial weighting and cap-
ture the indicator variability of different cities in different historical
stages, the system embeds a global entropy weighting engine based
on information theory.
4.3.1 Matrix Construction and Standardization
Construct the original evaluation matrixX={x i j}n×m, and use the
range standardization method to map data of different dimensions
to the[0,1]interval. To avoid zero values in subsequent logarith-
mic operations, perform a non-negative translation of 0.0001 on
the standardized results according to the suggestions ofSun et al.
(2012).
4.3.2 Weight Generation Pipeline
•Calculate information entropy:e j =−k ∑(Pi j lnPi j). The
larger the entropy valuee j, the smaller the difference of the
indicator among each station catchment, and the lower the
amount of information provided.
•Determine weights:Normalize the difference coefficient
d j =1−e j to obtain the indicator weightW j.
•Calculate scores:Obtain the subsystem scoresU 1 andU 2
respectively. This objective weighting method can effectively
capture the evolution of the importance of certain indicators
(such as population density in the early stage and POI mix in
the later stage) over long time series (Sun et al., 2012; Shen et
al., 2018).
4.4 Implementation of Coupling Coordination Degree
Model
This is the core logical step of the data pipeline, aiming to quantify
the interaction quality betweenU 1 andU 2:
•Coupling degree (C):Use the formulaC=

association between the two systems.
•Coordination index (T):SetT=αU 1 +βU 2, whereα=

•Coupling coordination degree (D):Finally calculateD=√C×T. TheDvalue takes into account both the associa-

4.5 Architecture Design and Engineering Execution of
the Data Pipeline
This pipeline is implemented using the Python geospatial technol-
ogy stack:
•Data extraction stage:The system traverses the annual
datasets from 1990 to 2025 and extracts active stations.
•Spatial calculation stage:Call osmnx to dynamically gener-
ate 15-minute walking isochrone polygons, and use rasterstats
to perform zonal statistics.
•Model calculation stage:Execute the entropy weight method
script to generate annual weights, and call the coupling algo-
rithm engine to output theDvalue of each station.
•Persistence and display stage:The calculation results are
stored in the PostGIS database in GeoJSON format, support-
ing dynamic rendering by the front-end through the timeline.

5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Design
To verify the effectiveness and practical value of the MetroCou-
ple system in the coupling analysis of urban rail transit and urban-
ization, this paper refers to the design research evaluation method
commonly used in the field of visual analytics (Sedlmair et al.,
2012) and combines the evaluation framework of InclusiViz (Yu
et al., 2025) in urban spatiotemporal visual analytics to construct
an evaluation scheme for urban planning scenarios. The evaluation
focuses on the system’s ability to support understanding, diagnosis,
and historical retrospective of complex spatiotemporal coupling re-
lationships.
The evaluation process plans to invite experts from two fields:
urban planning and visual analytics, to ensure that the system
not only meets planning business needs but also conforms to the
human-computer interaction logic of visual analytics. The overall
evaluation is carried out around three core dimensions:
Task completion effectiveness: Verify whether users can quickly
identify high-coupling areas, low-coordination areas, and the spa-
tial matching relationship between rail transit construction and ur-
ban expansion through the system.

5.2 Case Study: Shenzhen
Shenzhen is one of the most representative cities in China for TOD
practice. Its rail transit construction and urban spatial expansion
have highly synchronized development characteristics, which can
well reflect the application value of the system in “historical evalu-
ation” and “current status diagnosis”.
5.2.1 Coupling Characteristics of Rail Transit and Urban De-
velopment in Shenzhen
Through the system’s timeline retrospective function, it can be ob-
served that Shenzhen’s early rail transit construction was mainly
concentrated in the Luohu-Futian axis area, which was also the ear-
liest core area of high-intensity urban development in Shenzhen. It
has long shown high CCD values on MetroCouple’s coupling coor-
dination degree map, indicating that a good synergistic relationship
has been formed between rail transit construction and population,
economic, and functional development.
Further observation of the evolution process from 2000 to 2025
reveals that the coupling coordination degree of Shenzhen’s sta-
tion catchments shows an overall continuous upward trend. Es-
pecially after the gradual expansion of the rail transit network, a
large number of station catchments originally in a “barely coor-
dinated” state have gradually transformed into “well-coordinated”
and “high-quality coordinated” states, indicating that Shenzhen’s
urban development model has obvious “rail transit-led” character-
istics. That is, rail transit construction not only serves existing ur-
ban areas but also actively guides subsequent land development and
functional agglomeration.
The system’s long-term retrospective function also reveals an im-
portant evolution process of Shenzhen’s urban morphology: early
Shenzhen’s urban development mainly expanded in a belt shape
along transportation corridors. With the continuous densification of
the rail network, multiple high-coordination nodes have gradually
formed a stable urban group structure. It can be clearly observed
from the visualization results that rail transit lines have played a
key role in promoting the transformation of urban space from “lin-
ear expansion” to “multi-center grouping” (Pan, 2008).
5.2.2 TOD Characteristics Under Multi-City Comparison
In the horizontal comparison of the six case cities, Shenzhen shows
the highest overall coupling coordination level. Compared with
early rail transit cities such as Beijing and Shanghai (Dong et al.,
2013; Yao & Xiong, 2013), most core station catchments in Shen-
zhen maintain a synchronous growth state of highU 1 (rail transit
development index) andU 2 (urban development index), indicating
that its TOD strategy has achieved good coordination effects during
long-term implementation.
At the same time, the system also reveals a certain degree of
low coupling phenomenon in some newly built station catchments
on the periphery of Shenzhen. These areas usually show that rail
transit facilities have been laid out in advance, but population den-
sity, functional mix, and social vitality are still relatively insuffi-
cient, presenting a “transportation-led” characteristic ofU 1 ≫U 2.
This phenomenon is common in most rapidly expanding rail tran-
sit cities in China (Cheng, 2025), indicating that some edge areas
in Shenzhen have the problem of advanced rail construction and
temporarily mismatched functional configuration.
This finding further proves that MetroCouple can not only iden-
tify “successful TOD areas” but also effectively diagnose potential
spatial mismatch problems, providing a basis for subsequent land
development strategies and resource allocation optimization.
5.2.3 Verification of Design Goals
The Shenzhen case verifies two of the three core design goals pro-
posed in Chapter 3:
In terms of DG2 “dynamic retrospective of long-term coupling
evolution”, the system can clearly show the long-term impact of rail
construction on urban morphological evolution and support histori-
cal review of policy effects;
In terms of DG3 “transparent diagnosis of multi-source het-
erogeneous indicators”, the system can help users identify the
formation reasons of high-coordination areas and mismatched ar-
eas through station catchment portraits and subsystem contribution
analysis.
Therefore, MetroCouple can not only serve as a display tool
for rail transit development status but also as a visual diagnostic
platform supporting TOD historical research and planning decision
analysis.
5.3 Preliminary Expert Feedback
As the complete large-scale user evaluation is still in progress, this
study invited an expert with an urban planning background to con-
duct a preliminary experience and interview with the system proto-
type.

The expert recognized the intuitiveness and research value of
MetroCouple in the “visual expression of coupling relationships”,
believing that the system has successfully transformed the abstract
coupling coordination degree model into an observable and compa-
rable spatial evolution process, enabling researchers to more intu-
itively understand the interaction between rail transit construction
and urban development.
The expert particularly emphasized the potential of the system
in “model scalability”. The current system mainly uses a unified
station catchment radius for analysis, while in real urban planning
practices, different functional areas often require differentiated spa-
tial scales. For example, in Singapore’s TOD research, a core walk-
ing radius of about 300m is adopted for central business district
stations, while a multi-level station catchment range of 500 800m
is used for peripheral areas (Liu, 2017). The expert believes that
MetroCouple’s current visualization architecture has reserved good
expansion space for future multi-scale custom coupling analysis.
The expert also pointed out that the coupling coordination degree
model tends to identify potential opportunity points more easily in
regions with relatively balanced economic development. For ex-
ample, some U.S. rail transit studies prioritize areas with relatively
homogeneous economic structures for analysis to reduce the inter-
ference of spatial heterogeneity on coupling results.
In addition, the expert believes that the system’s continuous
recording of historical evolution processes has high academic value.
Through long-term accumulated time series data, researchers can
not only analyze the urban morphological evolution process but also
trace the relationship between policy implementation and rail con-
struction at different stages, providing a new analytical perspective
for TOD policy evaluation and urban development research.
6 DISCUSSION
6.1 Reducing the Cognitive Threshold of Coupling
Analysis Through Visualization
Under the traditional coupling coordination degree analysis frame-
work, planners need to have a solid quantitative analysis foundation
to understand the calculation logic and indicator composition of the
CCD model. This technical barrier has largely hindered the pop-
ularization and application of coupling analysis methods in actual
planning decision-making.
One of the core contributions of MetroCouple is transforming
complex coupling models into intuitive visual language. Through
the five-level color spectrum design, planners can judge the cou-
pling coordination level of a station catchment solely by color with-
out understanding the technical details of information entropy and
coupling degree formulas; through the hierarchical progressive de-
sign of the right indicator panel, the corresponding relationship be-
tween the comprehensive CCD value and subsystem scores is clear
at a glance; through the historical retrospective function of the time-
line, policy effect evaluation changes from static tables to dynamic
evolution animations. This “theory-driven visual transformation”
lowers the application threshold of coupling analysis methods.
6.2 System Limitations and Future Work
As a lightweight prototype system, MetroCouple focuses on the in-
tuitive communication of core indicators, but still has the following
limitations that need to be gradually improved in subsequent re-
search:
Data level: The current system uses population dynamic data
(LandScan), economic output data (GDP), and POI data with differ-
ent temporal resolutions. Among them, GDP data is only updated to
2020, and estimation errors are introduced through piecewise linear
interpolation completion.
Station catchment definition and variable radius model: The
system currently uses a unified 15-minute (900-second) walking
isochrone as the station catchment boundary. However, as pointed
out by experts, in actual TOD planning, differentiated analysis
radii should be adopted for city center stations and peripheral
stations—the former for refined evaluation with a 300m walking
circle, and the latter for evaluation with an extended radius of
500 800m. This feedback directly points out the limitation of the
current system’s “processing unbalanced cities with a unified ra-
dius”. Future work will explore a variable radius model that auto-
matically matches the analysis range according to station levels to
adapt to the functional area differences within different cities.
Customized coupling expansion of multiple factors: The cur-
rent system only covers four indicators: population, GDP, POI, and
building density. Experts proposed that the system should have the
ability to flexibly introduce new coupling factors according to dif-
ferent planning scenarios and research needs, such as industrial type
distribution, carbon emission intensity, and community service fa-
cility density. This demand points to the expansion potential of
the system as a “customized TOD analysis tool”: in the future, a
factor library and weight configuration module can be established,
enabling planners and urban researchers to select different factor
combinations for in-depth coupling analysis for specific research
objects.
Timeline animation presentation of urban morphological evolu-
tion: Although the current system’s timeline function can drive the
annual switching of map states, its visualization fluency and anima-
tion effects have not yet reached the presentation level required for
urban morphology research. Expert feedback indicates that if the
coherence and visual expressiveness of animations are enhanced
(such as adding transition animations and change highlighting), the
system will be able to more effectively support visual narrative and
policy review scenarios of urban morphological evolution laws.
Evaluation level: The current expert evaluation is still in the pre-
liminary stage, and larger-scale user experiments will be carried out
in the future to systematically verify the integration potential of this
system in the actual planning workflow.
7 CONCLUSION
This paper designs and implements MetroCouple, a visual analytics
system for urban rail transit and urbanization evolution across mul-
tiple cities. Taking the station catchment as the core spatial unit, the
system establishes a complete analysis process from macro trends
to micro causes through rasterized fusion processing of long-term
multi-source data and the CCD model.
The case study of Shenzhen verifies the practical value of the
system in identifying high-coupling station catchments, diagnosing
abnormal points, and retrospectively reviewing urban morphologi-
cal evolution. The horizontal comparison results of six cities show
that Shenzhen, as a typical rail transit-led city, has achieved remark-
able results in promoting the coordination between rail construction
and urban development through its TOD strategy.
In summary, MetroCouple provides an extensible research plat-
form for the visual analysis of the coupling relationship between
rail transit and urbanization through lightweight prototype design
and rigorous coupling models. The value of the system lies not
only in its current diagnostic functions but also in its long-term ap-
plication potential as an iterative tool for TOD planning.
